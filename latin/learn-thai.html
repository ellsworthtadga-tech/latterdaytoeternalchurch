Thai Tones

Thai has 5 tones. The same syllable said with a different tone can mean something completely different. This is the key to being understood!

กา
Mid Tone (สามัญ)
Flat, neutral pitch — like stating a fact calmly. กา (gaa) = crow
ก่า
Low Tone (เอก)
Pitch drops low and stays — like a disappointed "oh."
ก้า
Falling Tone (โท)
Starts high, drops sharply — like an emphatic "No!"
ก๊า
High Tone (ตรี)
Pitch rises high — like an excited question.
ก๋า
Rising Tone (จัตวา)
Dips low then rises — like a surprised "Really?"

Classic example: "ไหม" — mái (rising) = new, mâi (falling) = not, máai (high) = silk, mǎai (rising) = wood. Your wife can help you hear the differences!

Survival Tips
🙏 ครับ / ค่ะ — The Polite Particles

As a male, add ครับ (kráp) to the end of sentences to be polite. Your wife uses ค่ะ (kâ). This is the single easiest way to sound polite and respectful.

😊 Smile — It Goes Far

Thailand is called the "Land of Smiles." Even if your Thai is rough, a smile + attempt goes a very long way. Thai people genuinely appreciate any effort to speak their language.

🌶️ Ordering Food Hack

Say ไม่เผ็ด (mâi pèt) for "not spicy" or เผ็ดนิดหน่อย (pèt nít nòi) for "a little spicy." Thai spicy is no joke!

🗣️ No Verb Conjugation!

Thai verbs don't change for tense, person, or number. "Go" is always ไป (bpai) whether it's I go, he went, or we will go. Context does the heavy lifting.

👑 Pronouns Are Flexible

Use ผม (pǒm) for "I/me" as a male. With your wife you can use เธอ (ter) for "you" in a familiar way. In casual speech, pronouns are often dropped entirely.
